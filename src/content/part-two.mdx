# How Our Government Works

<p className="dot-rule sans text-xs uppercase tracking-[0.3em] text-gold/80 my-6">the design</p>

<div className="my-6 flex justify-center text-ink/80">

<DropCap>The U.S. government is set up so that no single person or group has all the power. The people who wrote the Constitution were worried about kings and dictators, so they split the government into three parts — called branches — and made each one able to push back on the others. That's what people mean by checks and balances.</DropCap>

<Chapter n="I" title="Congress · makes the laws" />

The <Word emoji="🏛" definition="The lawmaking branch. Two halves — House of Representatives (435 seats, by population) and Senate (100 seats, 2 per state). Both have to pass a bill before it becomes a law.">**Legislative Branch**</Word> is split into two halves, and a bill has to pass both before it can become a law:

- **The House of Representatives** — 435 people. How many each state gets depends on population, so California has a lot and Wyoming has very few. **2-year terms.**
- **The Senate** — 100 people. Every state gets exactly 2, no matter how big or small. **6-year terms.**

Congress also controls the money — they decide what the government spends on schools, the military, roads, everything. That's called <Word emoji="💰" definition="Congress controls how much money the government can spend and on what. Nothing else moves without it.">"the power of the purse"</Word>, and it's one of the biggest powers in Washington.

<Pull attribution="Why each state gets two senators">
  The Senate gives every state equal weight regardless of population. That's by design — small states wouldn't have agreed otherwise.
</Pull>

<GradientRule tone="terracotta" />

<Chapter n="II" title="The President · runs the country" />

<div className="my-6 flex justify-center text-ink/80">
  <Capitol size={150} />
</div>

The <Word emoji="🏢" definition="The President plus the Cabinet plus all the federal agencies. Job: enforce the laws Congress passes and run the country day-to-day.">**Executive Branch**</Word>'s job is to run the country day-to-day and enforce the laws Congress passes.

The President is elected every 4 years and can serve a maximum of **two terms**. The Vice President is the backup. Underneath them are the big departments — State (foreign affairs), Defense (military), Treasury (money), Justice (law enforcement), and a bunch more. The heads of those are called the **Cabinet**.

The President can also <Word emoji="🛑" definition="A presidential 'no' on a bill — blocks it from becoming law unless Congress overrides with a 2/3 vote.">veto</Word> bills from Congress, command the military, and make deals with other countries — though Congress has to approve the big stuff.

<GradientRule tone="sage" />

<Chapter n="III" title="The Courts · decide what it means" />

<div className="my-6 flex justify-center text-ink/80">
  <Gavel size={170} />
</div>

The <Word emoji="⚖️" definition="The courts — Supreme Court at the top, plus federal appeals and district courts. Job: decide what laws mean when there's a disagreement and strike down anything that violates the Constitution.">**Judicial Branch**</Word>'s job is to decide what the laws actually mean when there's a disagreement.

At the top is the **Supreme Court** — 9 judges (called **Justices**) who serve **for life**. When a law is unclear, or someone argues a law is unconstitutional, cases work their way up through lower courts until the Supreme Court has the final word.

The Supreme Court is powerful because it can strike down laws Congress passes and actions the President takes if they violate the Constitution. That power is called **judicial review** — and weirdly, it's not even in the Constitution. The Court claimed it for itself in 1803 (a case called *Marbury v. Madison*).

<Chapter n="IV" title="Federal vs. State" />

<div className="my-6 flex justify-center text-ink/80">
  <FederalState size={260} />
</div>

On top of all this, there are **two levels of government**:

- **Federal** — things that affect the whole country: military, currency, interstate commerce, immigration, treaties.
- **State** — most of daily life: schools, roads, marriage, traffic laws, criminal codes, property rules.

> Heads-up: <Word emoji="💇‍♀️" definition="The California Board of Barbering and Cosmetology — a state agency. Hair / nails / esthetics licensing is state-level, not federal.">cosmetology licensing</Word> is run by the **California Board of Barbering and Cosmetology** — a state agency, not federal. Most things you actually deal with as a small-business owner are state-level.

Some things, like **taxes**, you pay to both.
