# Part Two — How Our Government Works

The U.S. government is set up so that no single person or group has all the power. The people who wrote the Constitution were worried about kings and dictators, so they split the government into three parts — called **branches** — and made each one able to push back on the others. That's what people mean by <Word definition="The design where each branch of government has powers that limit the others — so no one branch can grab everything.">checks and balances</Word>.

## The three branches, at a glance

### Branch 1: The Legislative Branch (Congress)

**Job:** Make the laws.

Congress is split into two halves, and a bill has to pass both before it can become a law:

- **The House of Representatives** — 435 people. How many each state gets depends on population, so California has a lot and Wyoming has very few. They serve **2-year terms**.
- **The Senate** — 100 people. Every state gets exactly 2, no matter how big or small. They serve **6-year terms**.

Congress also controls the money — they decide what the government spends on schools, the military, roads, everything. That's called <Word definition="Congress controls how much money the government can spend and on what. Nothing else moves without it.">"the power of the purse"</Word>, and it's one of the biggest powers in Washington.

### Branch 2: The Executive Branch (The President)

**Job:** Run the country day-to-day and enforce the laws Congress passes.

The President is elected every 4 years and can serve a maximum of **two terms**. The Vice President is the backup. Underneath them are the big departments — State (foreign affairs), Defense (military), Treasury (money), Justice (law enforcement), and a bunch more. The heads of those are called the **Cabinet**.

The President can also <Word definition="A presidential 'no' on a bill — blocks it from becoming law unless Congress overrides with a 2/3 vote.">veto</Word> bills from Congress, command the military, and make deals with other countries — though Congress has to approve the big stuff.

### Branch 3: The Judicial Branch (The Courts)

**Job:** Decide what the laws actually mean when there's a disagreement.

At the top is the **Supreme Court** — 9 judges (called **Justices**) who serve for life. When a law is unclear, or someone argues a law is unconstitutional, cases work their way up through lower courts until the Supreme Court has the final word.

The Supreme Court is powerful because it can strike down laws Congress passes and actions the President takes if they violate the Constitution.

## How they check each other

This is the part that makes the whole thing work:

- Congress writes laws, but the President can **veto** them.
- If the President vetoes, Congress can **override** with a 2/3 vote.
- The President picks Supreme Court Justices, but the **Senate has to approve** them.
- The Supreme Court can rule that a law from Congress or an action from the President is **unconstitutional** — meaning it doesn't count.
- Congress can <Word definition="The formal process to remove a President or other high officer for serious misconduct. Charges in the House, trial in the Senate.">impeach</Word> a President or a Justice if they break the law badly enough.

**Nobody gets to run away with the whole show. That's the design.**

## Federal vs. State

On top of all this, there are two levels of government:

- **Federal** — handles things that affect the whole country: military, currency, interstate commerce, immigration, treaties.
- **State** — handles most of daily life: schools, roads, marriage, traffic laws, **cosmetology licensing**, criminal codes, property rules.

> Heads-up: cosmetology licensing is run by the **California Board of Barbering and Cosmetology** — a state agency, not federal.

Some things, like **taxes**, you pay to both.
