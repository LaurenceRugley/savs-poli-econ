# Three Economic Systems

<p className="dot-rule sans text-xs uppercase tracking-[0.3em] text-gold/80 my-6">the big three</p>

Every country has to answer three basic questions:

- **Who owns the stuff that makes other stuff?** (factories, land, salons, businesses)
- **Who decides what gets made?**
- **How is the money split up?**

The three big systems people argue about — **capitalism**, **socialism**, and **communism** — are different answers to those three questions. They're not three points on a line. They're three takes on the same handful of choices.

<GradientRule />

<Chapter n="I" title="Capitalism" />

<div className="my-6 flex justify-center text-ink/80">
  <SalonChair size={180} />
</div>

<DropCap>In capitalism, regular people and private companies own the businesses, the land, and the tools that produce things. If you open a salon, the chairs, the products, and the profits are yours. Prices and wages are mostly set by what people are willing to pay and what workers are willing to accept — that's what people mean when they say "the market."</DropCap>

**The upside:** people are free to start things, take risks, and keep what they earn. That tends to drive innovation — new businesses, new products, new ideas.

**The downside:** it can create big gaps between rich and poor, and the market doesn't automatically take care of things like healthcare, clean air, or people who can't work.

<Pull attribution="Capitalism, in a sentence">
  You buy the chairs. You set the prices. The clients pay you. What's left after rent is yours to keep or reinvest.
</Pull>

The United States is mostly capitalist. Your salon, our business, Amazon, the corner taco truck — all privately owned.

<GradientRule tone="terracotta" />

<Chapter n="II" title="Socialism" />

<div className="my-6 flex justify-center text-ink/80">
  <TipJar size={170} />
</div>

<DropCap>Socialism is a big umbrella. The basic idea is that some important things shouldn't be left purely to private owners and the market — the public should own them, or the government should make sure everyone has access. There's a soft version and a hard version, and most arguments online are people using one word for both.</DropCap>

### The soft version

Sometimes called <Word emoji="🌿" definition="A version of socialism that keeps private companies and markets but uses high taxes to fund a strong safety net — healthcare, parental leave, education, worker protections.">democratic socialism</Word>. It keeps capitalism but adds a strong safety net: free healthcare, free college, generous parental leave, strong worker protections. Companies are still private; the government just taxes more and provides more.

> Think **Norway, Denmark, Sweden**. They still have billionaires and Starbucks.

### The hard version

The government (or workers collectively) actually owns the major industries — the oil companies, the railroads, the big factories. Markets shrink, government planning grows.

**The upside:** less inequality, more shared services.
**The downside:** higher taxes, and when the government runs too much, things can get slow, inefficient, or politically controlled.

<GradientRule tone="sage" />

<Chapter n="III" title="Communism" />

<div className="my-6 flex justify-center text-ink/80">
  <OpenBook size={170} />
</div>

<DropCap>Communism is the most extreme version. There's a theory of it, and there's the practice of it. Almost every argument you'll hear online is people swapping the two.</DropCap>

### In theory

An end-goal society with no private ownership at all, no rich-and-poor classes, and no government needed because everyone shares everything based on need. That's the dream version a writer named <Word emoji="📖" definition="German philosopher and economist, 1818–1883. Wrote 'The Communist Manifesto' (1848) and 'Das Kapital' (1867). Almost every socialist and communist movement traces back to him.">Karl Marx</Word> described in the 1800s.

### In practice

Every country that's tried it — the old Soviet Union, China under Mao, Cuba, North Korea — ended up with the opposite of "no government": **one ruling party that controlled basically everything**, including what you could say or where you could work.

<Pull attribution="The whole confusion in one sentence">
  "Communism" in theory and "communism" in practice are very different things — and most online fights are people using the same word for both.
</Pull>

  <p className="prose-savs max-w-prose mx-auto">Two books, both by Karl Marx (with his friend Friedrich Engels). Almost every socialist and communist movement since then traces back to these two.</p>

- ***The Communist Manifesto*** (1848) — a short, fiery pamphlet. The famous closing line is *"Workers of the world, unite!"*
- ***Das Kapital*** (1867) — a much longer, denser book analyzing how capitalism works and what Marx thought was wrong with it.
