# Part One — Three Economic Systems

Every country has to answer three basic questions:

- **Who owns the stuff that makes other stuff?** (factories, land, businesses)
- **Who decides what gets made?**
- **How is the money split up?**

The three big systems people argue about — **capitalism**, **socialism**, and **communism** — are different answers to those questions.

## Capitalism

In capitalism, regular people and private companies own the businesses, the land, and the tools that produce things. If you open a salon, the chairs, the products, and the profits are yours. Prices and wages are mostly set by what people are willing to pay and what workers are willing to accept — that's what people mean when they say *"the market."*

**The upside:** people are free to start things, take risks, and keep what they earn. That tends to drive innovation — new businesses, new products, new ideas.

**The downside:** it can create big gaps between rich and poor, and the market doesn't automatically take care of things like healthcare, clean air, or people who can't work.

**Example:** The United States is mostly capitalist. Your salon, our business, Amazon, the corner taco truck — all privately owned.

## Socialism

<Word definition="Big umbrella word — covers everything from Nordic welfare states with private companies and billionaires, all the way to fully state-owned economies. Most arguments confuse these.">Socialism</Word> is a big umbrella. The basic idea is that some important things shouldn't be left purely to private owners and the market — the public should own them, or the government should make sure everyone has access. There's a soft version and a hard version.

### The soft version

Sometimes called **democratic socialism**. It keeps capitalism but adds a strong safety net: free healthcare, free college, generous parental leave, strong worker protections. Companies are still private; the government just taxes more and provides more. Think **Norway, Denmark, Sweden** — they still have billionaires and Starbucks.

### The hard version

The government (or workers collectively) actually owns the major industries — the oil companies, the railroads, the big factories. Markets shrink, government planning grows.

**The upside:** less inequality, more shared services.
**The downside:** higher taxes, and when the government runs too much, things can get slow, inefficient, or politically controlled.

## Communism

Communism is the most extreme version. **In theory**, it's an end-goal society with no private ownership at all, no rich-and-poor classes, and no government needed because everyone shares everything based on need. That's the dream version a writer named <Word definition="German philosopher and economist, 1818–1883. Wrote 'The Communist Manifesto' (1848) and 'Das Kapital' (1867). Almost every socialist and communist movement traces back to him.">Karl Marx</Word> described in the 1800s.

**In practice**, every country that's tried it — the old Soviet Union, China under Mao, Cuba, North Korea — ended up with the opposite of "no government": one ruling party that controlled basically everything, including what you could say or where you could work.

**The takeaway:** "communism" in theory and "communism" in practice are very different things, and a lot of arguments online happen because people are using the same word for both.

## Where did these ideas come from?

Two books, both by **Karl Marx** (with his friend **Friedrich Engels**):

- ***The Communist Manifesto*** (1848) — a short, fiery pamphlet. The famous closing line is *"Workers of the world, unite!"*
- ***Das Kapital*** (1867) — a much longer, denser book analyzing how capitalism works and what Marx thought was wrong with it.

Almost every socialist and communist movement since then traces back to these two books.

## Quick cheat sheet

- **Capitalism** — private people own businesses; markets decide prices.
- **Socialism** — public ownership or strong government services; aims to reduce inequality.
- **Communism** — in theory, no classes or private property; in practice, one-party state control.
